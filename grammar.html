Parts of Speech

The building blocks of English language:

  • Nouns: Person, place, thing, or idea (e.g., student, city, happiness)
  • Pronouns: Replaces a noun (e.g., he, she, they, it, someone)
  • Verbs: Shows action or state of being (e.g., run, is, become, think)
  • Adjectives: Describes a noun (e.g., big, blue, interesting)
  • Adverbs: Describes a verb, adjective, or adverb (e.g., quickly, very, extremely)
  • Prepositions: Shows relationship (e.g., in, on, under, between)
  • Conjunctions: Connects words or groups (e.g., and, but, or, because)
  • Interjections: Expresses emotion (e.g., wow!, oh!, oops!)
Example Sentence:

"Wow! The quick brown fox jumps over the lazy dog."

This sentence contains all eight parts of speech.

Sentence Structure

How words are organized to create meaning:

  • Simple Sentence: One independent clause (e.g., "She runs.")
  • Compound Sentence: Two independent clauses joined by a conjunction (e.g., "She runs, and he walks.")
  • Complex Sentence: One independent clause + one dependent clause (e.g., "She runs because she enjoys it.")
  • Compound-Complex: Two independent clauses + one dependent clause (e.g., "She runs every morning, and he walks because he prefers it.")
Sentence Analysis:

"Although it was raining, we went for a walk, and we enjoyed it."

This is a compound-complex sentence with:

  • Dependent clause: "Although it was raining"
  • Independent clauses: "we went for a walk", "we enjoyed it"

Tenses Overview

How verbs indicate time:

  • Present: Current actions or general truths
  • Past: Completed actions
  • Future: Actions that will happen

Each time frame has four aspects:

  1. Simple: Basic form (e.g., "I eat")
  2. Continuous: Ongoing actions (e.g., "I am eating")
  3. Perfect: Completed actions (e.g., "I have eaten")
  4. Perfect Continuous: Duration of actions (e.g., "I have been eating")

Subject-Verb Agreement

Verbs must match their subjects in number:

  • Singular subjects take singular verbs ("The dog barks")
  • Plural subjects take plural verbs ("The dogs bark")

Special cases:

  • Collective nouns can be singular or plural ("The team is ready" vs. "The team are arguing")
  • Indefinite pronouns like "everyone" are singular ("Everyone has left")
  • "Either/or" and "neither/nor" follow the closest subject ("Neither the students nor the teacher knows")

Articles

Words that define a noun's specificity:

  • Indefinite Articles (a, an): Used with nonspecific nouns ("I need a pen")
  • Definite Article (the): Used with specific nouns ("Pass me the salt")

Rules:

  • Use "a" before consonant sounds ("a university")
  • Use "an" before vowel sounds ("an hour")
  • No article with plural or uncountable general nouns ("I love dogs")

Modals

Special verbs that express necessity, possibility, or ability:

Can/Could: Ability or possibility ("I can swim")

May/Might: Possibility or permission ("It may rain")

Must: Necessity or strong recommendation ("You must see this")

Shall/Should: Suggestions or future ("Shall we go?")

Will/Would: Future or hypotheticals ("I would go if I could")

Characteristics:

  • No -s in third person ("She can swim")
  • No infinitive or participle forms
  • Followed by base verb ("She should go")

Conditional Sentences

If-then constructions for different scenarios:

Type Structure Example
Zero If + present, present If you heat water, it boils.
First If + present, will + base If it rains, we'll stay home.
Second If + past, would + base If I were rich, I'd travel.
Third If + past perfect, would have + past part. If I had studied, I'd have passed.

Passive Voice

When the subject receives the action:

  • Structure: Form of "be" + past participle
  • Use: When the doer is unknown or unimportant
Active vs Passive:

Active: "The chef cooked the meal." (focus on chef)

Passive: "The meal was cooked by the chef." (focus on meal)

Tense examples:

  • Present: "The letter is written"
  • Past: "The letter was written"
  • Future: "The letter will be written"
  • Perfect: "The letter has been written"
